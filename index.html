In Summary...

The UK's "first past the post" voting system, means that the candidates with the most votes in their constituency wins the seat in the House Of Commons.

In Charts 1-3 for the 2019 election results, we can see there are several parties with a lower ratio of votes to seats and vice versa but most notable are the Scottish National Party having less than half of the number of votes than the Liberal Democrats but 4 times the amount of seats and the Democrat Unionist Party has just a quarter of the amount of Lib Dem votes yet they hold only a few seats less. The Brexit Party had gained an overall 644k votes without a seat compared to The Alliance Party's 134k votes and 1 seat. Again, with only 115k votes, the SDLP gained 2 seats while Green gained only 1 with 865K votes. This could imply that parties with the lower seat to vote ratio may have a wider footprint of supporters across the United Kingdom rather than being concentrated in (a) particular region(s).

The next election may prove to make unusual history if we see these new parties gain traction with their campaigning. The pollsters have suggested that the "Conservative strength was pointed to 'leave-supporters'". Election survey data suggested similar turnout by age-group compared to previous elections.

Historically, we can see in Charts 4-5 that not much changed over the past century, with the power alternating back and forth between the Conservative and Labour parties, with the differences going from one extreme to the other in respect of one party's seat gain over the other.

Between 2001 and 2015 there was a much lower turn-out of voters and the elections were between 4 and 5 years apart. We can only speculate on the reason why and could be attributed to either indifference or a lack of urgency for change.

Project Purpose

This data visualisation dashboard/blog project on the UK General Elections, has served to provide me with a better understanding of our political history and contributed to some answers and insights that the raw data alone could not.

I am neither journalist, data scientist, writer; just a software development student at Code Institute who, at the time that my questions arose about which way to cast my vote in the 2019 elections, had just learned the basics of the wonderful ‘d3.js’, ‘dc.js’ and ‘crossfilter.js’ libraries and wanted to experiment with them some more and found this to be a great opportunity to explore both subjects.

At the very least, whether by chance or for reason, I hope that my visitors enjoy the presentation of data and I am optimistic that everyone may glean some insights, as I have done, from being able to discern the patterns and changes in the charts.

Final comment...

There are many new political parties on the block and some could be accused of making a mockery of the space governing the country. One cannot help but wonder how Monster Raving Looney Party secured 200% more votes than the Animal Welfare Party. Even though the oldest standing parties are still winning the power struggle, I think it begs the question - "What is politics coming to?"

England has the oldest parliament in the world, having met for the first time in the Palace of Westminster, or the Houses of Parliament as it's called today, in 1265.

Two parties were formed as a result of the constitutional crisis between 1678 and 1681, known as the Exclusion Crisis, namely 'Whigs' and 'Tories' and an interesting fact to note is that the descendants of these parties formed the coalition government between 2010 and 2015. I wonder what they would have had to say?
